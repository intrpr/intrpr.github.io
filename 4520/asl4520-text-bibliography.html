ASL-4520-X01 Deaf People and Disability Studies | Spring 2019

This online-only course is a seminar-like introduction and examination into the confluence of Deaf people and disability studies. Instructor/student interaction will be in both ASL and written English. Upon successful completion of this course, students should be able to demonstrate they can:

  1. explain how American society constructs disability and contrast this with perspectives from disability studies and Deaf studies
  2. describe historical, social, political, religious, philosophical, and cultural influences on both Deaf and disabled people
  3. describe the complex relationship between Deaf and disability rights groups
  4. explain how Deaf persons and persons with disabilities construct their own meanings and identities
  5. articulate ways that the study of Deaf people might inform disability studies’ theoretical underpinnings
  6. apply principles taught in the course to conduct self-analysis toward understanding societal constructs of disability and Deaf people

Additional Learning Objectives

In addition to the main learning objectives above, students should be able to demonstrate university-level ability in

  • expressing appropriate and expected civil netiquette in online conversations and commentary
  • articulating personal reasoning when comparing (the same) and contrasting (different) perspectives and observations
  • deducing, constructing, and expressing a research-informed opinion
  • producing academic ASL-styled presentations and accurate usage of APA referencing and citation form

As you know, this course requires your completely self-directed efforts (a/synchronously working with other colleagues) and fairly good command of web-based technologies and learning environments (video creation, salient discussion, and reporting skills).

Arguably, the most important skills in an online-only class are communication intent and language. More attention is required when you don’t have a simple way to clarify your intent or a statement you make. Additionally, there is no tone or affect in your online communication so word choice, grammar, and punctuation are crucial. Here are some principles of ‘netiquette,’ or communicating effectively in our interactions.

In your communication with classmates, please:

  • Clarify and support with evidence the position you take in your answer or reply.
  • Suggest ways in which an idea could be more clearly expressed.
  • Avoid absolutes. ‘Never,’ ‘always,’ ‘every,’ ‘only,’ ‘none,’ and ‘must’ are examples of absolute language and they typically cause others to be defensive (‘Mary always says that in her posts,‘ ‘You can’t be right because Einstein never made any mistakes...‘) Think twice before including them in a response in a discussion.
  • Identify specific passages or sentences where you think the writer misunderstood a concept or applied it incorrectly (“David wrote in his post that Einstein theorized about water and mass, but on page 14, Einstein doesn’t use water in his theory....”).
  • Disagree with a point made in an answer (“I think Einstein was talking about mass, not gravity...”), not the other person (“Mary was wrong when she said Einstein was talking about...”).

It’s okay to disagree with another person’s views, but please:

  • Be constructive and respectful in your disagreement.
  • Politely critique a position on an issue, not the person.
  • Avoid sarcasm, profanity, or informal language that would be considered rude or argumentative.
  • State precisely the point you disagree with (“I agree with Mary’s first two points, but I don’t quite see the third point about Einstein in the same way. I’ll explain....”)
  • Offer reasons why you think a person’s view is incorrect and always support your position by citing the text or other sources.

Ground Rules for Course Discussions, Email, and Internet Postings

  1. Participate. Please participate in the shared learning environment, avoid lurking in the background. It is not enough to log in and read the discussion thread of others. For the maximum benefit (and grade), everyone is intended to contribute.
  2. Report glitches. Discussion forums are electronic; they can break. If you experience difficulty participating, please contact or inform your instructor of the issue so that it can be corrected as soon as possible.
  3. Help others. If you have more experience with online discussion forums than another classmate, please help. Be a good citizen and others will appreciate it. Karma.
  4. Be patient. Please read everything in the discussion thread before replying. This will help you avoid repeating something that someone else has already contributed. Recognize the concepts and ideas with which you agree and suggest alternatives for those with which you don’t.
  5. Be brief, but answer the question. Be clear in your word choice and articulate your point, but don’t be preachy or pompous. Be direct, stay on point, and don’t lose yourself — or your classmates — in wordy sentences or paragraphs.
  6. Use proper writing and citation style. This is important; write more like a term paper and less like a text message (Hemingway app can help with this). Correct spelling, grammatical construction, and sentence structure are expected in every other writing activity associated with scholarship and academic engagement. Online discussions are no different.
  7. Cite your sources. Also crucial; if your contributions to the conversation include authored material of others (e.g., books, videos, or journal articles, whether online or in print), give the proper attribution. The citation style for Deaf Studies is APA; there are several helps to using APA style, but I can recommend Brigham Young University Linguistics and Northen Michigan University Olsen Library as good resources.
  8. Emoticons and text language. Don’t confuse online courses with social media and text messaging. ”kewl :-),” ”nvm,” and “c u l8r” are not part of the academic dialogue. Please avoid.
  9. Respect diversity. Be sensitive to the multicultural, multi-able, multilingual community in which we are participating. Please avoid language that is — or might be considered — offensive toward others. (Pro tip: if you think something might be offensive, it probably is. Time away and/or a second opinion can be really helpful.) Racist, hetero/homosexist, and derogatory or sarcastic commentary directed at religious beliefs, disabilities, and age is unacceptable. Rich debate is encouraged about topics in which we might disagree, but word choice must always be measured. Thanks.
  10. No YELLING! This probably goes without saying, but using bold and/or uppercase letters is the equivalent of stomping around and yelling at somebody (NOT TO MENTION BEING HARD ON THE EYE).
  11. Refrain from flaming and rants. We’ve kind of stated this in numbers 4, 9, and 10 above, but to reiterate, criticism is acceptable and encouraged if required, but it must be constructive, well-meaning, and well-articulated. We’re college students; no tantrums. Rants and profanity directed at other contributors are simply unacceptable and may result in assignment (and even course grade) penalty.
  12. You can’t ‘un-ring the bell.’ As I said at the beginning, language is arguably your only tool in an online environment. Step carefully. How others perceive you will be decided by your interaction. Once you’ve clicked ‘Submit’ or ‘Send,’ you’ve rung the bell. Re-read your post before you ‘Send.’ This will tell you a lot about whether your grammar and sentence structure are correct, your tone is appropriate, and your contribution clear or not.

Always review your written posts and responses before you submit to ensure that you’ve conveyed exactly what you intended. This is an excellent opportunity to practice proofreading, revision, and rewriting skills — all valuable assets in the professional world for which you are now preparing.

    Having trouble putting words and sentences together? The Hemingway Editor is a solid helpful tool. Here are some fairly common problems to watch for in your paper:

    1. Awkward sentence structures. Avoid fragments and run-on sentences. “The Hartford school helped refine ASL. And standardize it too.” is a fragment.
    2. Incorrect punctuation. Don’t misuse commas and semicolons. Semicolons only link two related—but independent—sentences together.
    3. Incorrect use of it’s. It’s = It is (“It is a tragedy.”); its = belonging to ‘it’ (“Its process was way off.”)
    4. Incorrect use of quotation marks and italics. Book, play, poem, periodical, film/TV titles should be italicized, not in quotation marks.

    “There aren’t enough books or references.”
    Nothing could be further from the truth.     The assignment doesn’t say every reference or source must be laser-focused on your chosen topic, just the majority of them.     Arguably, the most important skills in an online-only class are communication intent and language. More attention is required when you don’t have a simple way to clarify your intent or a statement you make. Additionally, there is no tone or affect in your online communication so word choice, grammar, and punctuation are crucial. Here are some principles of ‘netiquette,’ or communicating effectively in our interactions.

    In your communication with classmates, please:

    • Clarify and support with evidence the position you take in your answer or reply.
    • Suggest ways in which an idea could be more clearly expressed.
    • Avoid absolutes. ‘Never,’ ‘always,’ ‘every,’ ‘only,’ ‘none,’ and ‘must’ are examples of absolute language and they typically cause others to be defensive (‘Mary always says that in her posts,‘ ‘You can’t be right because Einstein never made any mistakes...‘) Think twice before including them in a response in a discussion.
    • Identify specific passages or sentences where you think the writer misunderstood a concept or applied it incorrectly (”David wrote in his post that Einstein theorized about water and mass, but on page 14, Einstein doesn’t use water in his theory....”).
    • Disagree with a point made in an answer (“I think Einstein was talking about mass, not gravity...”), not the other person (“Mary was wrong when she said Einstein was talking about...”).

    It’s okay to disagree with another person’s views, but please:

    • Be constructive and respectful in your disagreement.
    • Politely critique a position on an issue, not the person.
    • Avoid sarcasm, profanity, or informal language that would be considered rude or argumentative.
    • State precisely the point you disagree with (“I agree with Mary’s first two points, but I don’t quite see the third point about Einstein in the same way. I’ll explain....”)
    • Offer reasons why you think a person’s view is incorrect and always support your position by citing the text or other sources.

    Signer Canvas

    Presentations should be filmed in front of a contrasting background (darker for Caucasian people; lighter for People of Color) with contrasting clothing and appropriate personal styling. Many of the personal styling guidelines for Academic ASL presentations are the same for interpreters:

    • contrasting clothing devoid of exaggerated patterns (floral patterns, contrasting repetitive [i.e., houndstooth or plaids] patterns, tartans, flannels, or ginghams)
    • clothing styling that either demonstrates professionalism or matches the formality of the setting
      • professional clothing that does not create an undue or unprofessional visual distraction
    • minimizing alterations to one’s personal appearance that impact a signer’s nonmanual marker production and viewer’s comprehension:
      • minimal to no shiny or dangly jewelry, especially no lip, nose, or eyebrow piercings
      • minimal or neutral makeup, including prudence in eyeliner, mascara, and lipstick density and color
      • minimal to trimmed fingernail length, shape, and decoration with minimal/neutral/nude coloring

    Lighting and Positioning

    Front lighting should appropriately highlight manual movements and facial expressions but not blow out the signer’s facial features nor create a chiaroscuro effect; backlighting should be removed or heavily minimized. Backgrounds should be solid and devoid of contrasting patterns.
