Comparative ASL / English Language Continuum

This is a comparative bilingual continuum for use with Deaf and Interpreting Studies students that identifies the diglossic and representative relationships of artificial manual codes of English (MCEs) derived between American Sign Language (ASL) and English. It also identifies additional gestural MCEs that are English-derived but that may also be used with deaf people. (Although the content is representative of the latest printed version (2019), this code is still in development; see this project's changelog and roadmap.)

This HTML/hypertext version () supercedes any of the popular printed versions in that 1) it is more easily updated/distributed and 2) allows for additional footnoted and documented hypertexted media (research/text, video, etc.). This version also supercedes the version printed in HumphreyH, J., ClarkH, L., FeatherstoneD, J., and Ross IIIH, W. F. (2020). You want to be an interpreter (5th edition). Vancouver, WA: H&H Publishing.

All implied placements and relationships are relative; overlapping approaches drawn for distinction and not for accuracy. (Subscripts on surnames (e.g., D or H) identify that an author, researcher, contributor, or creator is Deaf or hearing.)

Feedback, addition, and corrections are always welcome.

⇠ MCE approach

Conceptually Accurate Signed English (CASE)

Popular alternative to Signed English, sometimes called PSE (Pidgin Signed English). Combines ASL signs and semantics in an MCE form. Not a language.

MCE approach ⇢

Sign Supported Speech (SSS)

Voicing spoken English and signing an MCE. Sometimes called Simultaneous Communication (SimCom) or Total Communication (TC). Not a language.

1

American Sign Language (ASL)

Visio-gestural, preferred, and connate language of Deaf people of the United States and Canada. Etymology and roots in:

  • Indigenous , regional , unrecorded, and unknown SLVs
  • Native American SLVs (ClarkH, W., 1885; DavisD, 2007)
  • Black SLVs (HillD, J., McCaskillD, C., BayleyH, R., & LucasH, C., 2015; McCaskillD, C., LucasH, C., BayleyH, R., & HillD, J., 2020)
  • British/ Kentish/ Vineyard SLVs (GroceH, 1985; c.f., KitzelH, M. E., 2013)
  • Mexican SLVs (Quinto-PozosH, 2002; 2008)
  • French SLVs (PelissierD, 1856; LaneH, 1984; )
First documented in BrownH, 1856 and 1860 (StringhamH, 2019). Topic-comment grammatical structure and approach (cf. Baker-ShenkH & CokelyH, 1980); utilizes code-borrowing from majority spoken language. Also called ‘the sign language’ (VeditzD, 1913) and ‘Ameslan’ (FantH, 1972).

2

Signed English (SE)

Simplified English-based code; only fourteen added grammatical markers.

(Developed mid-1970s, Dr Harry BornsteinD, Gallaudet College; 1983, Bornstein, Saulnier, & Hamilton)

3

Seeing Essential English (SEE1)

(Formerly ‘SEE1’); intended to reinforce basic English morphemic structure:

  • compound words are formed with separate signs (‘butter’+‘fly’)
  • same/one sign used for homonyms (‘bear’ and ‘bare’)
  • heavy use of initialization (haVe)
  • affixes, articles, and ‘to be’ (copula) verb added

(Developed 1966, David AnthonyD, Gallaudet College)

4

Signing Exact English (SEE2)

(Formerly ‘SEE2’); similar to SEE1 but:

  • compound words are conceptually accurate (‘butterfly,’ not ‘butter’+ ‘fly’)
  • more ASL signs (one sounded word = one sign)
  • at least seventy artificial and invented signs and affixes added to this system

(Developed 1972, Dr Gerilee GustasonD)

5

Linguistics of Visual English (LOVE)

Visual recording system based on Seeing Essential English (SEE1). Used Stokoe Notation System (tab-dez-sig; Stokoe, 1960; StokoeH, CasterlineD, & CronebergD, 1965) to codify. Defunct.

(Developed 1972, Dennis WamplerD)

6

Rochester Method

Each lexical unit produced using the manual alphabet. Extensively used in schools for the deaf in the late 19th century. Sometimes used in tactile/deaf-blind signing situations; some Deaf adults still use this method. Parodied in deaf literature and narratives.

(Developed 1878, Zenas WesterveltD, Western New York Institute for Deaf-Mutes, later Rochester School for the Deaf)

9

Cued Speech (CS)

Not a signed language. Combines eight arbitrary handshapes and four locations to visually and phonetically represent English. Cued speech video sample and Cued Speech Association video samples. Learning to cue: Lesson 1 and Lesson 2 with a deaf CS user.

(Developed 1966, Dr Robert CornettH, Gallaudet College)

10

Paget-Gorman Signed Speech (PGSS)

Not a signed language. British-based pantomimic, morphological based manual system. First experimental teaching of the system in 1964 with a group of deaf adults; PGSS first conceptualized in Britain in 1934 as a systematic pantomimic sign language whereby each word would have its own sign, with the signs presented in the same sequence as the words in the phrases or sentences to be signed. Similar to Makaton, now used primarily with people who have aphasias and speech disabilities.

(Developed 1934, Sir Richard PagetH)

11

Makaton

Not a signed language. Originally researched by Margaret Walker in the early 1970s and added vocabulary development in the mid-1980s to work with cognitively disabled deaf adults. Later and currently adapted to work with communicatively disabled hearing children and adults. Base vocabularies originally derived from British Sign Language (BSL) but adapted to work locally/regionally by deriving signs and gestures from other BANZSLs and other national SLs and their variants.

(Research c. 1972–1973; vocabulary development 1985–1986. , Margaret WalkerH, Botley Park Hospital, Chertsey, Surrey, UK
